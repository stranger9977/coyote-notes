PASTA
Path Analysis via Swarm-Tackle Accuracy

Grades how well a defensive secondary "swarms" to the ball to limit yards after catch — inspired by DeMeco Ryans' swarm philosophy, built with swarm intelligence (Particle Swarm Optimization).

How it works

  • Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO): treat each defender as a particle; the objective is to minimize distance to a "swarm point" T on the ball-carrier's path.
  • Target selection (T): the best reachable point along the carrier's path, scored by each defender's distance and pursuit angle (points no one can reach given a min velocity → score ∞, removed).
  • Error = Fréchet distance between a defender's actual path and the PSO-suggested path (the "leash length" between the two curves).
  • The metric: PASTA = YUX / Fréchet distance, where YUX = Yards-after-catch Under eXpected (Expected YAC − Actual YAC). Higher = stayed near the optimal path and limited YAC.

What it found

  • Fréchet distance correlates negatively with YUX — secondaries closer to the optimal path give up less YAC. CBs have the largest Fréchet (they cover the most field).
  • Team board: Titans #1, DeMeco Ryans' 49ers top-5 — both hustle-minded staffs, hinting at a coachable component.

Assumptions to defend

The PSO path is "optimal." PSO is stochastic (random terms) and the params are unprincipled — inertia = the golden ratio, φ₁=φ₂ untuned, Vmin = the carrier's average velocity. It's a defensible physics-of-reachability heuristic; I'd tune the params, use player-specific speed profiles, and report path stability across random seeds.
A single static target on a moving ball-carrier. The runner cuts and reacts; one fixed point oversimplifies the pursuit (I flagged this as a limitation). Move to a moving-target PSO so T updates as the carrier's path develops.
Fréchet distance = "error." It's a worst-point path-shape measure — it ignores timing/speed and can't tell a mistake from a justified deviation (a DB peeling off to cover a checkdown looks like "error"). Add a time-aligned or area-between-curves measure and condition on the defender's actual assignment.
PASTA = YUX ÷ Fréchet is an unstable ratio. It blows up as Fréchet → 0, flips sign when YUX is negative, and divides a play outcome by a path metric. Use a standardized/regression form, winsorize, or model YUX additively with path-deviation as a predictor.
Correlation, not causation. The YUX↔path link is only moderate; good defenses may both follow good paths and limit YAC (talent confound) — path-following might be a symptom, not the cause. Control for coverage, defender talent, and play context; test whether path-deviation predicts YUX within player/team.
Scope & dependencies. Secondary only, pass plays only, weeks 1–9 of 2022; YUX inherits any error in nflfastR's expected-YAC model; reliability/year-to-year stability isn't shown. Expand the sample, run split-half reliability, and stress the xYAC dependency.

CAMO
Coverage Assignment Misclassification Ownership

Measures how much each defender contributes to disguising the coverage pre-snap — by training a transformer to read coverage and mining its attention on the plays it gets fooled.

How it works

  • Transformer over the 22 players as a set (6 features each: x, y, vx, vy, orientation-to-QB, team side) → classifies 1 of 8 coverages each frame (adapted SumerSports framework). Adding QB orientation alone lifted accuracy 7–10%.
  • Accuracy: line-set→ball-snap window ~79–83% top-2; by the forward pass ~92–94% — strong enough that remaining misses are plausibly designed disguise.
  • The metric: CAMO = MR × (player's attention share) × 100, where MR = the play's misclassification rate. It's the share of the model's attention a player draws on plays where the defense fooled it.

What it found

  • Bills top the misclassification board (fool the model 56.7% of pre-snap frames). Top player Andre Cisco (22.7% of his team's fooled frames). Spagnuolo's Chiefs top-3 — good face validity.
  • Motion lowers misclassification — it helps the offense expose coverage; elite (low-CPOE) offenses fool the model more.

Assumptions to defend

Misclassification = designed disguise. The core leap: the model being fooled ≈ the defense successfully disguising. But the model can just be wrong/limited (capped by data & labels), not deceived. Lean on face validity (good DCs rank high) and the orientation ablation — manually spinning a safety tanks accuracy, so the model keys on disguise-relevant cues. Then validate against coach-tagged disguise plays.
Attention = responsibility. Assumes the transformer's attention faithfully says who caused the disguise — but attention isn't always a faithful explanation. I already ablate (orientation spin); add integrated-gradients / attention-rollout checks and validate against ground truth before calling it "ownership."
Human-labeled, 8-bucket coverage. Accuracy is capped by label quality, and real coverages don't fit cleanly in 8 boxes. Acknowledge the ceiling; report top-2 accuracy; treat fuzzy labels as a noise floor, not model failure.
Eight weeks of 2022 data. Small, single-season — generalizability is unproven, and the model struggles in crowded box areas. Expand seasons; check stability; flag box-heavy plays as lower-confidence.
MR × attention mixes levels & only credits misses. A play-level rate times a frame-level attention share; players who disguise on correctly-read plays get nothing. Report components separately; consider crediting attention on "near-miss" (low-margin) correct plays too.
Does disguise actually help the defense? The link to offensive outcomes (CPOE) is indirect/correlational. Tie CAMO to EPA/CPOE allowed within matchups to show disguise has real value, not just model confusion.

DRAFT SHARPE
Risk-adjusted draft value — a Sharpe ratio for the NFL draft

Treats a draft pick like an investment: the "return" is rookie-contract surplus + the second-contract the market gives him; the "risk-free alternative" is just signing a starter in free agency. The ratio = expected value over that alternative, per unit of bust risk.

The formulas

Rookie Surplus = Snap Ratio × (FA Replacement Cost − Rookie Salary) × 4 yrs Player Return = 2nd-Contract Cap% + Rookie Surplus Sharpe = ( P(elite) × Elite Threshold − FA Replacement Cost ) / SD(Player Return)

Worked example — "is it all percentages?" → yes

Everything is in cap % (percent of the salary cap) except P(elite), which is a probability (0–1). So the units line up: cap% − cap% = cap%, then ÷ cap% (the SD) = a unitless ratio. That's why you can subtract "FA cost" from "P(elite) × threshold" — both are cap%.

Step 1 — one player's return · Josh Allen (QB, pick #7, 2018)

  • Snap ratio = 92.1% snaps ÷ hit threshold = 1.48× (≥1.0 = starter)
  • Rookie surplus = 1.48 × (14.6% FA cost − 3.0% rookie pay) × 4 yrs = 68.7%
  • Player return = 68.7% surplus + 23.6% 2nd contract = 92.3%

Read it: a top-10 QB returns +0.37 of risk-adjusted value over signing a free agent; a top-10 RB is −0.62 — you'd be better off buying one.

What it found

  • Premium positions win round 1: QB leads the top-10 (0.37); EDGE peaks in the late first (0.59); OT and WR also strong. RB is the worst first-round bet — negative at every tier (−0.62 in the top 10).

Counter-analysis (Part 2 — I stress-tested it myself)

  • Roster need: picks that fill a real hole average a need-adjusted Sharpe of +0.057 vs −0.139 for already-filled positions — context matters.
  • Class depth: using NGS pre-draft scores, count "Good+" prospects per class (depth ↔ hit rate, r = 0.42). 2026 RB is the thinnest in years — Jeremiyah Love is the only Elite RB, a 17-pt gap to RB2.
  • Prospect talent: pre-draft elite prospects (top-10% NGS) become starters 53.5% vs 13.5%. The averages hide the studs.
  • Hot take: Love's 90th-pct outcome crosses the premium-surplus line ~pick 14; the Vikings at 18 are the cleanest fit. For everyone else, the rule holds: draft a premium position.

Assumptions to defend

Snap % = quality. Playing time is a volume proxy — a player can play a lot and be bad. Swap to PFF grades or EPA for production-weighted returns.
Second contract = market value, and FA pool includes extensions. Teams over/underpay, and counting re-signings inflates "replacement cost" for positions stars rarely leave (OT, QB). Use true open-market (team-change) signings only; track how often starters actually change teams per position.
A 4-year window, and $0 for no second contract. Misses year-5+ late bloomers and zeroes out players who contributed but aged out (esp. RBs). Extend the window / use position-specific development curves; give partial credit for rookie-deal production.
One global "elite" bar & uniform class talent. A 90th-pct cutoff across all positions, and every class treated as equally talented. Position-specific thresholds + draft-class strength controls (the Part-2 NGS depth work does this).
Cap% is the cross-era comparator. It ignores structural cap jumps (new TV deals). Adjust for cap-growth between eras.
QB is treated like any position. The math says QB is the best top-10 bet "on average" — but NCAA→NFL projection is brutally hard and the bust variance is enormous. Apply an explicit QB bust-rate discount; the SD already partly captures it, but make the variance explicit. (The math still favors taking the swing.)

Strong framing for the room: I don't just list these — Part 2 already addresses three of them (roster need, class depth, prospect talent). "I anticipated the obvious objections and built the counter-analysis" is a better answer than any single defense.
