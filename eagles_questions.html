Interview day · reverse interview

Questions for them

Good questions show you're already thinking like you have the job. Listen as hard as you ask.

My openers

  1. Who's your favorite pick from this year's draft class, and why? Are there any Day 3 picks you're especially excited about — guys who could make an outsized impact? Signals you've studied the class; their answer tells you how the building actually values traits vs. production, and how Day-3/UDFA bets get made.
  2. What's your favorite under-the-radar free-agent signing this offseason — a low-cost add you think could outperform the price tag? Pairs with the draft question and is very Eagles — value-hunting at the bottom of the roster. Surfaces how they think about cheap upside (the Ebiketie / Pierce / Moore-type bets).
  3. What's the most challenging part of the job? Invites candor and shows you want the real picture, not the brochure. Listen for whether it's technical, organizational, or buy-in.
  4. What's your favorite part of the job? Warm, human, and tells you what energizes the team you'd join.

The role & the day-to-day

  1. What does a typical week look like in-season versus the offseason for this role? Sets expectations and shows you're thinking about fit, not just the title.
  2. What does success look like in the first 6–12 months — how will you know I'm doing well? One of the strongest questions you can ask; gives you a concrete target and shows ownership.
  3. How's the time split between long-horizon research projects and quick, same-day questions from coaches or the front office? Reveals whether it's a deep-work shop or a fire-drill service desk.
  4. What does the analytics stack look like today — languages, the tracking-data pipeline, modeling tools — and where are the biggest gaps you'd want help closing? Shows you'll be useful Day 1 and surfaces where you can add value fast.

How analytics drives decisions

  1. Can you walk me through a recent decision where analytics genuinely changed the outcome — and maybe one where the data got overruled, and why? The single most revealing question about how much the work actually matters here.
  2. How does analytics reach the people making the calls — is the group embedded with coaches and scouts, or more of a central service? Tells you how your work travels and whether you'll see it land.
  3. Where do you see the biggest untapped edge right now — tracking data, special teams, in-game decisions, draft/roster modeling? Shows strategic thinking and teases out where they want to grow (and where you'd fit).
  4. How do you measure the impact of the analytics group itself? Sharp, slightly meta — shows you care about outcomes, not just outputs.

Collaboration & culture

  1. How do analysts, coaches, and scouts actually work together — and how does someone earn buy-in from football people who are skeptical of numbers? Communicating to non-technical decision-makers is the whole game; asking this shows you get that.
  2. When "the model says X" collides with football intuition, how does the team handle it? Reveals the culture's maturity around analytics — and your own humility.
  3. How would you describe the culture of the group and the building? Simple, honest, and the answer's tone tells you a lot.

Growth

  1. How has this role evolved over the last few years, and where do you see it heading?
  2. What separates the analysts who thrive here from the ones who struggle? Gets you the unwritten success criteria.

Show you know ball (use 1–2, don't force it)

  1. How heavily is player tracking / Next Gen Stats used today, and is there appetite to build counterfactual, "ghosting"-style player-evaluation metrics in-house? Ties straight to your COYOTE prep — signals you're already operating in their problem space.
  2. Special teams is famously under-measured — how much analytics attention does it get, and is there room to push there? A pointed, on-brand question (it's literally what COYOTE tackles).
  3. How do you think about build-vs-buy between third-party models (PFF, Sumer, Next Gen) and proprietary in-house work? Shows you understand the practical economics of an analytics shop.

Scheme & trends

  1. The league feels like an arms race right now — defenses going two-high and disguising who rushes versus who drops (the Fangio / Macdonald / Flores wave), and offenses answering with record pre-snap motion and heavy 12-personnel to punish those light boxes. With Fangio's system on defense and all the tight ends the team added this offseason, how do you see those trends shaping how the Eagles build the roster and call games — and where's the next edge? Shows you track scheme at a league level and connect it to this roster (Fangio + the TE haul). Opens a real conversation, not a yes/no.
  2. Are defenses starting to solve pre-snap motion yet, or is the offense still winning that battle? A sharp, current follow-up — signals you read the chess match, not just box scores.

Defense — the disguise era

  • Two-high shells + quarters (Cover-4/6) ~25% of coverages, rising — "functional double coverage" to erase explosive plays; the trade-off is a lighter box. Fangio is the godfather of the two-high movement.
  • Coverage disguise ~30% of dropbacks — show one shell pre-snap, rotate post-snap; make the QB hold it.
  • Pressure ≠ blitzing. Macdonald's Seahawks blitz ~22% (among the lowest) and win with a 4-man rush + stunts/twists; he insists he runs few true "sim pressures." The opposite pole — Brian Flores's Vikings — blitz and simulate at record rates ("rush from everywhere"). Two legit philosophies.
  • Simulated pressures / "creepers" — show blitz, still rush four, drop a lineman or edge into coverage to wreck the protection ID.
  • Base personnel comeback 23.5% → 29.6% — coverage-capable LBs to match heavy 12-personnel without going small (Lions ~82% base on 1st down).
  • Man coverage down to 22.6% (from over a third) — stop isolating LBs on fast WRs; lean zone-match.

Offense — bigger, and in motion

  • Pre-snap motion at record highs 64% of plays; up every year since 2014's 37.5% — reveals coverage, leverage and intent pre-snap.
  • Motion-at-the-snap rising Dolphins ~56% in '24 — turns a static look into a leverage race at the snap; stresses zone rules.
  • Heavy 12 personnel highest since 2000 22.1% of snaps; TEs ~9.2 YPA vs safeties — punish light two-high boxes and blur run/pass from one look. ← exactly why the Eagles loaded up on TEs.
  • Condensed / bunch sets + wide-zone & play-action (Shanahan / Mannion lineage) — rubs, leverage, easy motion.
  • RPOs declining 8.1%, lowest since 2019 — defenses got disciplined vs conflict defenders. Designed-QB-run stress remains (and the tush-push, rules permitting).
  • Eagles D = Fangio (back for year 3): the two-high, light-box, disguise, low-blitz, kill-explosives archetype — led the NFL in passing TDs allowed & opponent completion % in 2025. Roster fits it: length/speed CBs (Woolen), versatile DBs (Mitchell/DeJean), cheap pass-rush juice (Greenard, Ebiketie) so a 4-man rush wins.
  • Eagles O = Sean Mannion (new OC, Shanahan tree, 5th OC in 5 years): expect wide zone, motion, play-action — and a heavy-12 lean off the TE haul (Goedert, Stowers, Mundt, Smartt, Calcaterra) — straight out of the "punish light boxes" trend.

Where analytics is going

  1. Optical "pose" tracking is about to add limb-level data — full-body skeletons (Hawk-Eye's SkeleTrack reports ~29 points per player) instead of one dot. Where do you think that pays off first: technique & scheme evaluation, injury prevention, or player development? And how are you preparing for it? Shows you know what's literally being installed in the stadiums right now, and that you think in use cases, not just data.
  2. In baseball, analytics jumped from evaluating players to developing them — pitching labs adding velocity and inventing pitches. Is football using data that way yet, to actually develop technique, or mostly for injury prevention and load management? How big a part of the job is that here, and where do you see it heading? The pitching-lab analogy is a strong vision signal — it reframes analytics as a development engine, not just an evaluation tool. Listen for how forward-leaning they are.

Beyond tracking dots — the frontier

  • Pose / skeletal tracking. Hawk-Eye/Sony 32-camera arrays are now in every stadium; SkeleTrack captures ~29 body points per player, many times/sec — tested in the background in 2025, not yet shared with teams. The leap: from one centroid dot → full-body biomechanics (pad level, hand placement, hip/ankle angles, contact force). (You may hear higher point counts quoted; ~29 is the publicly reported Hawk-Eye figure.)
  • The "pitching lab" analog for football. Baseball (Driveline / Trackman) turned data into development — velo gains, pitch design. Football's version is just emerging: biomechanics for OL hand/footwork, pass-rush mechanics, route technique, kicker/punter motion, sprint mechanics. Pose data is the unlock.
  • Injury prevention & load management. The league-wide Digital Athlete (NFL + AWS) fuses pads-sensor + optical tracking + ML to flag risk; teams use GPS wearables (Catapult), the acute:chronic workload ratio, change-of-direction load, and sleep/recovery to manage soft-tissue risk and return-to-play.
  • Beyond on-field tracking. Computer vision on All-22 (auto-charting technique/leverage), richer contextual models, draft / roster / cap optimization, AI-assisted opponent scouting. The edge is shifting from "who's good" → "why — and how do we make them better."

Closers

  1. Is there anything about my background that gives you pause that I could speak to right now? Bold, and it lets you address doubts before you leave the room.
  2. What are the next steps — and is there anything I can send over that would be helpful?
If you only have time for a few: the draft-pick question (you'll have it from the Offseason tab), "what does success look like in 6–12 months," "a decision analytics changed — and one where it got overruled," and "how do you earn buy-in from skeptical football people." Then close with the "anything that gives you pause" question.
