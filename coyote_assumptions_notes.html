COYOTE — assumptions in the write-up & how I'd address them

Interview notes. Each item: the assumption baked into the paper → what's wrong → the fix.

1. Player ordering — the field is a sorted flat vector transformer fix

Model A centres on a “target player”; both models then list the other players sorted by distance (nearest → furthest) inside one flat feature vector.

  • This forces an arbitrary, unstable ordering. When two players swap distance-rank mid-play, their features jump to different input slots, so the net sees a sudden discontinuity even though nothing meaningful changed — and a player’s identity / role is thrown away by the sort. The net also has to re-learn the same interaction separately at every slot pairing.
  • A transformer (self-attention over the 22 players) doesn’t care about order, and here’s why: it treats the players as a set, applies the same learned weights to every player (weight-sharing), and pools them with a symmetric operation (mean) → permutation-invariant (reshuffle the players and the output is identical). So it needs no sort — it learns who matters via attention (softmax(query·key)), dynamically each frame, instead of relying on a fixed distance ranking.

2. Frozen field state — the counterfactual holds the other 21 players constant

  • COYOTE swaps the target player to his “average” spot while freezing everyone else — a partial-equilibrium counterfactual. In reality the other 21 would react (a defender re-routes around a missed block; a blocker adjusts), so COYOTE can mis-credit reactions and blocker synergy. The better-but-harder version is a reactive multi-agent simulator (re-roll the play with all players responding via Model A). Is it worth it / is it fine? As a per-frame approximation it’s genuinely defensible — over a single 0.1 s step the field barely moves, so freezing introduces little error. The error compounds over long sequences, so it’s an acceptable v1, but a reactive sim is the clear upgrade if you want a true causal counterfactual and to capture synergy between blockers.

3. Arbitrary threshold — Model A uses only the 5 nearest players transformer fix

  • Model A’s 42 features = target + 5 nearest neighbours. That hard top-5 cutoff is really a scaling hack to bound input size, and it discards anyone past #5 — e.g. a defender closing from the far side who’s about to decide the play. A transformer removes the cap: feed all players and let attention learn who’s relevant (a soft, learned “nearest”), so it can care about a player on the opposite side of the field exactly when he matters.
    • This improves both models — a more accurate Model A (movement informed by the whole field, not 5 neighbours) and a more accurate Model B — and it pays off most once Model B predicts quantiles / a distribution: capturing the rare far-field defender or the decisive seal is what lets us zero in on the blocks and players that spring explosive (tail) returns, not just the median play.

4. The 3-yard halo (ε = 3)

  • When ghosting the target, COYOTE also replaces every player within a 3-yard radius of him with their Model-A “average” position — the idea being to isolate the effect of the immediate halo around the player while holding the rest of the field constant. The 3-yard radius is an unprincipled hand-set threshold: too small misses the real interaction zone of a block, too large contaminates the swap with unrelated players. (Separate but related 3-yard rule in the code: COYOTE is zeroed when the player is within 3 yds of the ball carrier, which is what keeps it an off-ball metric — it never credits the carry/tackle itself.) A learned/soft influence region — or just using the attention weights as the “halo” — would be more defensible than a flat 3.

5. Point-estimate ghost & mean-only Model B → ghost a distribution 2025 BDB idea

Today Model A outputs a single expected position (one ghost point) and Model B is trained with MSE → a single expected-yards number. Problems: value is nonlinear, so plugging in the mean position ≠ the mean of the value (Jensen bias: V(mean) ≠ E[V]); one ghost point can be unrealistic; and you get no uncertainty. The recent Big Data Bowl idea (Yurko / Ferraz “ghosting” with distributions): don’t place one ghost — model a probability distribution over where an average player would be and integrate the value model over it.

  • Ghost a distribution, not a point. Have Model A emit a distribution over the next position (predict a mean + covariance, a mixture, or use a small generative model), then compute COYOTE = f(actual) − Eghost[ f(field with target sampled from the ghost-distribution) ] by sampling many ghost positions and averaging Model B. This de-biases the counterfactual (kills the V(mean)≠E[V] error) and gives confidence intervals on each COYOTE/ret instead of a bare number.
  • Pair it with a distributional Model B (quantiles). Swap MSE for a quantile / pinball loss so Model B predicts the distribution of remaining yards (τ0.1 floor … τ0.9 upside), not just the mean. Combined with ghost-sampling, COYOTE can then measure a player’s effect on the tails — i.e. who tilts the probability of an explosive return — the boom/bust signal the point-estimate version averages away.
  • How the field actually did it (prior art): Yurko et al. — “NFL Ghosts” (2024) use random-forest conditional density estimation (RFCDE) to model a full 2D distribution of where the baseline (“ghost”) defender should be — plus a second density for receiver yards — then grade a real defender by integrating the value model over that ghost density (E[V], not V(mean)). Ferraz — “Ghostbusters” (Big Data Bowl 2026 winner) applies the same distributional-ghost idea to defenders while the ball is in the air, scoring coverage by comparing actual movement to a distribution of expected ghost positions. So the ladder is: COYOTE = the cheap end (point ghost + mean) → Yurko / Ferraz = the distributional upgrade → a reactive ghost (others respond) is the rung above both.

But Model A outputs 5 numbers — how do you get a distribution out of that?

The 5 outputs (Δx, Δy, accel, x-vel, y-vel) are just the predicted mean — one point. The count of outputs isn't the obstacle; you change what the head emits (and the loss). Cheapest → richest:

  • Re-head to a Gaussian. Instead of "the next position," output its parameters: a mean and a spread per coordinate — e.g. μx, μy, σx, σy (+ correlation ρ). Train with Gaussian negative-log-likelihood instead of MSE, then sample positions from N(μ, Σ). Your 5 point-outputs simply become distribution parameters.
  • Quantile head. Output a few quantiles per coordinate (τ = 0.1 / 0.5 / 0.9) with pinball loss → a spread, no Gaussian assumption.
  • Mixture Density Network. Output a mixture of K Gaussians (weights + means + covariances) → captures multimodal futures (a defender could break left or right) a single Gaussian can't.
  • Cheapest, no re-head — dropout / ensemble. Keep the point net, run it many times with dropout on (or train a small ensemble); each pass gives a slightly different 5-output → an empirical cloud of ghosts. (Caveat: that's the model's own uncertainty, not the true play-to-play spread.)
  • Yurko's actual choice: RFCDE — a random forest that emits a full conditional density directly, with no neural head at all.

Then the COYOTE step is identical for all of them:

  1. Sample N ghost next-positions for the player → N ghost field states.
  2. Run Model B on each → N expected-yards numbers.
  3. Average them = Eghost[f(Ŝ)] (that's the "integrate"); their spread = the confidence interval.
  4. COYOTE = f(actual) − Eghost[f(Ŝ)].

Averaging f over where the player could be — instead of f at one average spot — is exactly what removes the Jensen bias.

6. Punts and kickoffs are pooled into one model

  • One Model B is trained on both return types, but punts and kickoffs are different regimes — different geometry, blocking schemes, and returner depth — and ~62% of frames are kickoffs, so the punt signal gets drowned out. Split into two models (or condition Model B on play type) so each return type is graded on its own dynamics.

7. All return-team blockers are ranked in one bucket — split by role

  • COYOTE/ret lumps every blocker together, but roles differ enormously — on punts especially, a jammer / vise pressing the gunner at the line does a fundamentally different job than a downfield punt-return blocker springing the returner. Ranking them on one leaderboard is apples-to-oranges. Grade within role (role-specific leaderboards) to make ratings fair, more interpretable, and to surface undervalued specialists a pooled ranking would hide.
